{\ttfamily parse-\/latin} splits text into white space, word, and punctuation tokens. {\ttfamily parse-\/latin} starts out with a pretty easy definition, one that most other tokenizers use\+:


\begin{DoxyItemize}
\item A “word” is one or more letter or number characters
\item A “white space” is one or more white space characters
\item A “punctuation” is one or more of anything else
\end{DoxyItemize}

Then, it manipulates and merges those tokens into a (\href{https://github.com/syntax-tree/nlcst}{\texttt{ nlcst}}) syntax tree, adding sentences and paragraphs where needed.


\begin{DoxyItemize}
\item Some punctuation marks are part of the word they occur in, such as {\ttfamily non-\/profit}, {\ttfamily she’s}, {\ttfamily G.\+I.}, {\ttfamily 11\+:00}, {\ttfamily N/A}, {\ttfamily \&c}, {\ttfamily nineteenth-\/ and…}
\item Some full-\/stops do not mark a sentence end, such as {\ttfamily 1.}, {\ttfamily e.\+g.}, {\ttfamily id.}
